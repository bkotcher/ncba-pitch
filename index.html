REASON 01 · THE ADJACENCY

Wealth is the return NCBA isn't capturing.

NCBA runs a 20.8% ROE bank with EC$1.4B in assets and an investment book larger than its loan book — but earns nothing on its clients' investment wealth, which sits offshore. Wealth management is the single highest-return adjacency available, and it requires no balance-sheet risk: it is fee income on assets NCBA already has the relationships to capture.

REASON 02 · BUILD VS. PARTNER

The platform takes years to build. We have it.

NCBA is filing for a broker-dealer license — the right move. But a license is not a wealth business. The platform, the products, the risk engine, the regulated rails: that is 18–24 months and millions to build alone. Sub-advisory gives NCBA the full stack on day one, while NCBA keeps the client and the economics.

REASON 03 · THE WINDOW

Three forces are open right now.

  • Consolidation: the largest regional wealth players are mid-integration and distracted — clients are in motion and dislike transitions.
  • Regulatory timing: NCBA's BD application is in flight — the structure can be built into it now, not retrofitted later.
  • New inflows: Anguilla's residency-by-investment program is delivering HNW arrivals today, with no investment home.

NCBA always owns the client, the license, the balance sheet, and the brand. The mass-affluent tier looks like RBC Investments; the sophisticated tier looks like RBC Dominion Securities — but both run on the same engine, with the same owner of record. This is the model that scales to every local bank in the region without ever competing with them.

Layer 03 — continuous adversarial stress testing
Daily · automated · constructed against each client portfolio
ADVERSARIAL STRESS · CORE IDEA

The system attacks itself every night.

Overnight, an automated agent constructs the worst plausible market scenario for each Premier portfolio — not from a static catalogue, but by solving for the combination of factor moves (equity beta, rate, FX, credit spread, vol) that maximises the next-day loss on that specific holding mix. The worst-case scenario is logged. If the resulting loss exceeds 12% of NAV, the portfolio is flagged for CIO review the next morning.

  • Approach: reverse-engineered stress vs. canned scenarios
  • Factor model: 60-day OLS, SPY + sector-residualised betas
  • Output: per-account vulnerability score & the worst-case factor combination
CONVENTIONAL STRESS SCENARIOS

Plus the canon, for board reporting.

In parallel, every portfolio is stress-tested against five canonical scenarios for the board pack:

  • 2008 financial crisis replay (−37% equity, +200bp credit spread)
  • 2020 March COVID shock (−34% equity in 23 sessions)
  • 2022 inflation cycle (rates +300bp, equity −18%)
  • USD-shock scenario (DXY +10%, EM/intl −12%)
  • Caribbean peg-stress scenario (XCD −5% hypothetical)

INTEL 01 · REPUBLIC × SCHRODERS

Republic's partnership template, and why it can't be copied.

Republic's wealth product is sub-advised by Schroders out of London/Singapore. It's the closest analog to the Premier structure regionally. But Republic gives up ~75% of the management fee to Schroders because Schroders is the brand and the manager — Republic is just the distributor.

  • Republic distributor share: estimated 25–30 bps on ~150 bps gross
  • Premier proposal: NCBA distributor share is 40 bps on 100 bps gross — 40%
  • NCBA earns proportionally more, on a cheaper client product
INTEL 02 · CIBC OFFSHORE PROBLEM

The Cayman routing problem CIBC can't solve.

CIBC FirstCaribbean's wealth product is domiciled in Cayman because they couldn't get regional approval to operate it from Bridgetown. This means CIBC's Anguilla branches refer wealth clients out of country — and OECS-supervised regulators don't have inspection rights over their accounts.

  • NCBA depositor referred to CIBC wealth → account in Cayman
  • NCBA depositor referred to Oceanic → account in Anguilla under ECSRC
  • Compliance posture materially differs
INTEL 03 · BOSL CEILING

Bank of Saint Lucia's wealth product hit a ceiling.

BOSL Asset Management launched Caribbean Money Market Funds in the early 2000s under ECSRC supervision. It scaled to several hundred million USD and stalled — because it remained money-market only. HNW clients wanting equity / multi-asset exposure left for offshore competitors.

  • BOSL's gap: no multi-asset capability under ECSRC license
  • Premier proposition fills exactly this gap — multi-asset, ECSRC-supervised
  • The model is proven; the product gap is not yet filled
INTEL 04 · THE WHITE SPACE

Where the OECS regional wealth gap actually is.

No firm in the region currently offers: OECS-supervised, multi-asset, co-branded with a local bank, with HNW client minimums of US$250k. Every competitor fails at least one of those criteria. Premier is built around all four.

  • OECS-supervised — Premier yes, CIBC/RBC/Butterfield no
  • Multi-asset — Premier yes, BOSL no
  • Co-branded with local bank — Premier yes, all others no
  • US$250k minimum — Premier yes, Republic+Schroders is US$500k
