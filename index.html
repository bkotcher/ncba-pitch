ECCB Financial Sector Assessment alignment
How AUA disclosure fits the regional supervisory framework
The technical compliance position

The ECCB's Financial Sector Assessment framework requires member-bank wealth-management activities to be (a) disclosed as off-balance-sheet AUA, (b) supervised under a recognised securities regulator (ECSRC qualifies), and (c) covered by client-disclosure rules around investment risk. This partnership satisfies all three conditions by construction — AUA disclosure is the standard regional treatment, Oceanic is ECSRC-licensed, and client onboarding includes ECSRC-prescribed risk-disclosure language. There is no novel regulatory question requiring ECCB pre-approval; the structure follows the same template First Citizens, RBC Caribbean, Scotia, and others have used for years.

EQUITY STAKE · INDICATIVE TERMS

25% stake · board seat · fair-valuation strike.

If NCBA decides during Year 2 that the partnership is strategically core, an equity stake in Oceanic is available on pre-defined terms. This converts NCBA from distribution partner into part-owner — with a share of Oceanic's full P&L, not just the distribution fees on NCBA-introduced AUM.

  • Stake size: 25% non-controlling
  • Valuation: independent fair-value report (Big-Four or equivalent)
  • Governance: one NCBA-appointed board observer or director
  • Accounting: equity method (NCBA reports proportional share of Oceanic net income)
  • Term: standard ROFR & tag-along provisions; no forced exit before Year 5
STRATEGIC RATIONALE

Why this option exists.

Two possible NCBA strategic answers to "how do we own wealth long-term":

  • Option A — distribution-only: partnership remains arms-length, NCBA keeps 100% of its capital optionality
  • Option B — equity stake (this section): NCBA accumulates proportional ownership of a regulated regional wealth manager — one that NCBA cannot easily build itself

Both options are written into the Year-1 partnership agreement. NCBA chooses in Year 2 with full information.

On the deposit-to-AUA migration — the CFO's question

A dollar invested in equities cannot simultaneously remain on NCBA's deposit ledger. Deposits migrate to AUA — this is unavoidable accounting, not a structural choice. Every Caribbean bank with a wealth offering — First Citizens, Bank of Saint Lucia, RBC Caribbean, Scotia Caribbean — accepts this trade because the alternative is worse: the client takes the entire deposit to an offshore competitor that already offers managed investments.

Under this partnership, NCBA retains the client relationship (KYC, primary banking, credit products), earns 40 bps of a 100 bps management fee on migrated AUM, and grows reportable franchise via AUA disclosure. The total economic value to NCBA exceeds the spread on the original deposit, with no incremental capital cost and no regulatory burden.

ADVERSARIAL STRESS · CORE IDEA

The system attacks itself every night.

Overnight, an automated agent constructs the worst plausible market scenario for each Premier portfolio — not from a static catalogue, but by solving for the combination of factor moves (equity beta, rate, FX, credit spread, vol) that maximises the next-day loss on that specific holding mix. The worst-case scenario is logged. If the resulting loss exceeds 12% of NAV, the portfolio is flagged for CIO review the next morning.

  • Approach: reverse-engineered stress vs. canned scenarios
  • Factor model: 60-day OLS, SPY + sector-residualised betas
  • Output: per-account vulnerability score & the worst-case factor combination
